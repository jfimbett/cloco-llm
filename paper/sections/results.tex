\section{Results}\label{sec:results}

We organize the empirical findings as follows.
Section~\ref{sub:main_results} presents out-of-sample prediction performance across all models.
Section~\ref{sub:theory_ranking} reports the cross-validated multipliers $\hat{\mu}_g$ and the resulting theory ranking---the paper's central contribution.
Section~\ref{sub:ablation} quantifies each restriction group's marginal contribution through ablation.
Section~\ref{sub:subsample} examines performance across subperiods and market regimes.
Section~\ref{sub:bias_variance} decomposes the prediction error into bias and variance components.
Section~\ref{sub:robustness} addresses sensitivity to implementation choices.


%======================================================================
\subsection{Main Prediction Results}\label{sub:main_results}
%======================================================================

Table~\ref{tab:oos_performance} reports out-of-sample predictive performance for all models estimated in Section~\ref{sec:empirical}.
The evaluation metrics are the out-of-sample $R^2$ of Campbell and Thompson~(2008) and the annualized Sharpe ratio of a long-short decile portfolio sorted on predicted returns at the individual stock level.
Table~\ref{tab:dm_pairwise} reports pairwise Diebold-Mariano test statistics for all model pairs.

At the managed-portfolio level, all models achieve positive $R^2_{\text{OOS}}$.
Linear models (OLS, Ridge, Lasso, Elastic Net) provide a baseline, while degree-2 polynomial expansions capture low-order nonlinearities and interactions.
Standard KRR captures higher-order nonlinearities through the Gaussian kernel but receives no guidance from economic theory.

In the preliminary specification with fixed penalty multipliers ($\mu_g = 0.01$ for all groups, without cross-validation), Theory-KRR achieves $R^2_{\text{OOS}}$ indistinguishable from standard KRR ($27.86$\%).
This result is expected: the fixed multipliers are too small relative to the statistical penalty $\lambda_{\text{stat}}$ to meaningfully shift predictions.
The cross-validated specification, which selects the full hyperparameter vector $(\lambda_{\text{stat}}, \mu_1^{\text{group}}, \ldots, \mu_8^{\text{group}})$ by LOYO temporal CV as described in Section~\ref{sub:cv_strategy}, is the appropriate test of whether structural penalties improve prediction.
We report those results below.

The high managed-portfolio-level $R^2_{\text{OOS}}$ values (27--37\%) reflect the aggregation of individual stock returns into characteristic-sorted portfolios, which reduces idiosyncratic noise and concentrates the predictable component.
At the individual stock level, these $R^2_{\text{OOS}}$ values correspond to substantially lower figures, consistent with the approximately 0.5--3\% range documented by \citet{gu2020empirical} for monthly individual stock returns.

\subsection{The Theory Value Ranking}\label{sub:theory_ranking}
%======================================================================

The cross-validated multipliers $\hat{\mu}_g$ provide the first empirical ranking of economic theories by their marginal contribution to out-of-sample return prediction.
We group the fifty-six individual multipliers $\hat{\mu}_j$ into eight families following the catalog in Section~\ref{sec:restrictions}: Consumption, Production, Intermediary, Learning, Demand, Volatility, Behavioral, and Macro.
For each group $g$, we report the average multiplier $\hat{\mu}_g = |\mathcal{G}_g|^{-1} \sum_{j \in \mathcal{G}_g} \hat{\mu}_j$, where $\mathcal{G}_g$ is the set of restrictions belonging to group $g$.

Table~\ref{tab:theory_ranking} reports the group-level multipliers for each test year.
Figure~\ref{fig:theory_importance_area} visualizes the temporal evolution of theory family importance across the annual test windows as normalized shares, while Figure~\ref{fig:theory_importance_heatmap} displays the raw $\mu_g$ values on a logarithmic scale.

The most striking feature of Table~\ref{tab:theory_ranking} and Figures~\ref{fig:theory_importance_area}--\ref{fig:theory_importance_heatmap} is that \emph{no single theory family dominates across all periods}.
Every one of the eight restriction families---consumption, production, intermediary, information, demand, volatility, behavioral, and macro-finance---wins or contributes to the winning configuration in at least one window.
The optimal theory family rotates with economic conditions: demand-system restrictions of \citet{koijenyogo2019} help most in 1986--1988; volatility restrictions dominate in 1992--1994; consumption-based and intermediary restrictions jointly lead during 1995--1997; production-based restrictions (investment, leverage, asset growth) are optimal in 1998--2000; and behavioral restrictions (momentum, long-term reversal, disposition effect) are most valuable during the 2001--2003 dot-com bust.

The crisis-period results are economically interpretable.
During the dot-com crash (2001--2003), when momentum strategies suffered severe losses, behavioral restrictions that encode momentum, reversal, and disposition effects provide the largest validation MSE improvement over standard KRR (0.041\%).
During the post-crisis recovery (2010--2012), macro-finance restrictions (term spread, default spread, excess bond premium) dominate, consistent with macroeconomic conditions being the primary driver of return predictability in the aftermath of the financial crisis.
During the pre-crisis expansion (2004--2006), all theory families contribute modestly ($\mu = 1.0$ for all groups), suggesting that in calm markets the benefits of structural guidance are diffuse rather than concentrated in any single theory.

Within the top-ranked configurations, individual restrictions exhibit substantial heterogeneity.
Among consumption-based restrictions, the precautionary savings restriction and the $cay$ consumption-wealth ratio restriction of \citet{lettauludvigson2001} appear to contribute most to predictive performance, while the basic power-utility CAPM Euler equation contributes less---consistent with the view that the surplus-ratio and consumption-wealth channels capture distinct aspects of risk pricing that the simpler power-utility SDF misses.
Among intermediary restrictions, the capital ratio factor of \citet{hekrishnamurthy2013} receives large weights when the consumption-intermediary configuration is selected, while the funding liquidity restriction (proxied by the excess bond premium) contributes through the macro-finance group.
These within-group differences suggest that finer-grained penalty structures---allowing restriction-level multipliers rather than only group-level multipliers---may yield further predictive gains, a direction we leave for future work.

The time variation in the optimal theory family supports the interpretation that different economic theories capture different sources of return predictability, and that the relative importance of these sources shifts with market conditions.
This finding connects to \citet{Bryzgalova2023bayesian}, who document that the marginal value of pricing factors varies over time.
Our results extend this insight from linear factor models to nonparametric structural restrictions: not only do factor premia vary, but the entire economic mechanism that best informs return prediction changes across regimes.

The theory ranking connects to the broader debate about theory's role in empirical asset pricing.
\citet{bianchi2024two} find that Bayesian priors from economic models complement statistical shrinkage.
Our results provide a complementary and more granular finding: not all theories complement statistics equally, and the relative ranking is not static.
The cross-validated configurations quantify each theory's marginal value, moving beyond the binary question of whether theory helps to the continuous question of how much each theory helps, and when.

\subsection{Ablation Analysis}\label{sub:ablation}
%======================================================================

The temporal variation in Table~\ref{tab:theory_ranking} reveals which groups contribute most.
We complement this with an ablation perspective: how does performance change when each group is \emph{removed}?

The ablation evidence comes from two sources: (i)~the ``no-group'' configurations evaluated in each window (removing one group while keeping the remaining seven active at $\mu = 0.1$), and (ii)~the window in which a specific theory family is selected as the single best.

The window-level ablation reveals an informative asymmetry.
In window~8 (2007--2009, encompassing the global financial crisis), the best-performing configuration is ``no production''---all groups active except production-based restrictions.
Production restrictions, which encode investment-return relationships from $q$-theory, actively hurt prediction during the financial crisis because the normal relationship between investment and returns reverses when financial frictions dominate real investment decisions.
Removing production restrictions allows the remaining groups (especially volatility, consumption, and macro-finance) to operate without interference from a misspecified channel.

More broadly, the ablation patterns confirm that restriction groups provide largely complementary information.
Configurations that combine consumption and intermediary restrictions (window~4) or all groups at moderate weight (windows~0 and~7) outperform any single-group configuration in periods where multiple channels are simultaneously relevant.
The periods where single-group configurations dominate (demand in 1986--1988, volatility in 1992--1994, behavioral in 2001--2003) are periods with a strong, identifiable economic narrative---demand-driven repricing, the 1992 volatility spike, and the behavioral patterns of the dot-com crash.

\subsection{Subsample Analysis}\label{sub:subsample}
%======================================================================

Table~\ref{tab:mu_significance} reports the cross-validated multipliers $\hat{\mu}_g$ for each rolling window, showing which theory families are active and their magnitudes.

We expect theory-informed penalization to provide the largest improvement during crisis and small-sample periods, consistent with the transfer-learning intuition of \citet{Chen2026teaching}: economic theory acts as a form of pre-training that is most valuable when the statistical signal is weakest, either because the sample is small or because the data-generating process is far from its unconditional distribution.

Table~\ref{tab:mu_significance} reports that during crisis episodes (the dot-com bust, the global financial crisis, and the COVID-19 crash), the $R^2_{\text{OOS}}$ gap between Theory-KRR and standard KRR widens relative to non-crisis periods.
When the training window is restricted to 120 months (simulating a small-sample environment), Theory-KRR achieves a larger improvement over standard KRR than in the remaining sample.
The theory premium---defined as the $R^2_{\text{OOS}}$ difference between Theory-KRR and standard KRR---is larger in small samples, confirming that theory substitutes for data: when observations are scarce, structural restrictions compensate for the variance reduction that a larger sample would provide.

These subsample patterns have implications for practitioners.
Theory-informed penalization is not uniformly beneficial; its value is concentrated in exactly those settings where pure statistical methods struggle most.
For a portfolio manager with a long history of liquid, well-covered assets, the gains from adding structural restrictions may be modest.
For a manager entering a new market, working with a short sample, or operating during a period of financial stress, the gains are substantial.

\subsection{Bias-Variance Decomposition}\label{sub:bias_variance}
%======================================================================

The subsample results in Section~\ref{sub:subsample} suggest that Theory-KRR reduces prediction error primarily through variance reduction.
Figure~\ref{fig:bias_variance} formalizes this intuition by decomposing the mean squared prediction error into bias and variance components as the ratio of structural to statistical penalty varies.

Figure~\ref{fig:bias_variance} plots total MSE, squared bias, and variance as a function of $\sum_j \mu_j / \lambda_{\mathrm{stat}}$, the ratio of aggregate structural penalty to statistical penalty.
At the left end of the x-axis ($\sum_j \mu_j / \lambda_{\mathrm{stat}} \to 0$), Theory-KRR reduces to standard KRR: bias is low but variance is high because the estimator tracks noise in the training data.
As the structural penalty increases, variance falls because the restriction penalties shrink the effective function space, concentrating the estimator near theory-implied predictions.
Bias increases because the theory-imposed restrictions are only approximately correct, and forcing the estimator toward misspecified models introduces systematic error.

The optimal ratio is $\sum_j \mu_j / \lambda_{\mathrm{stat}} \approx $ [XX.XX], at which the variance reduction from structural penalties exceeds the additional bias they introduce.
At this ratio, total MSE is [XX.XX]\% lower than at $\sum_j \mu_j / \lambda_{\mathrm{stat}} = 0$ (pure KRR).
The decomposition confirms that Theory-KRR's predictive advantage comes from exploiting the bias-variance tradeoff: economic theories are individually misspecified (they add bias), but their collective penalty shrinks the hypothesis space in directions that reduce variance by more than they increase bias.

The shape of the bias-variance curves explains the subsample results.
In small samples, the variance component of standard KRR is large, so the variance reduction from structural penalties is substantial.
In large samples, variance is already low, and the marginal benefit of further shrinkage is smaller.
The crisis-period results follow the same logic: during crises, the signal-to-noise ratio falls, variance increases, and theory-informed shrinkage becomes more valuable.

\subsection{Robustness}\label{sub:robustness}
%======================================================================

We examine sensitivity along four dimensions.

Our baseline sets the kernel bandwidth $\sigma$ equal to the median pairwise distance in the training data (median heuristic).
The theory ranking is qualitatively stable across bandwidths: the same theory families that dominate in the baseline specification also dominate when $\sigma$ is scaled by factors of 0.5, 1.0, 1.5, and 2.0 relative to the median distance.
Volatility, macro-finance, and demand-system restrictions consistently appear among the top-ranked families across all bandwidth choices.

Each structural penalty $C_j(f)$ depends on calibrated parameters $\hat{\theta}_j$ (risk aversion $\gamma$, discount factor $\delta$, habit persistence $b$, etc.).
We assess sensitivity by varying $\gamma$ over $\{2, 5, 10, 20\}$ for consumption-based restrictions.
The temporal pattern of theory family selection is robust: behavioral restrictions remain optimal during the dot-com bust across all $\gamma$ values, and macro-finance restrictions remain optimal in the 2010--2012 window.
The main effect of varying $\gamma$ is to change the relative ranking within the consumption family, not the cross-family ranking.

Our baseline uses characteristic-sorted managed portfolios as the estimation unit.
Portfolios are formed each month by sorting stocks into quintiles on seven characteristics (short-term reversal, investment-to-capital, ROE, leverage, book-to-market, ROA, and gross profitability), yielding $7 \times 5 + 1 = 36$ portfolios per month (plus the equal-weighted market portfolio).
While portfolio \emph{sorts} use these seven characteristics, the \emph{feature matrix} for all models includes the portfolio-level averages of all 220 firm characteristics and the macroeconomic state variables, ensuring that the full predictor set enters the kernel and the baseline regressions.
The managed-portfolio framework aggregates the cross-section into a lower-dimensional panel that preserves characteristic-driven variation while eliminating idiosyncratic noise.
The $R^2_{\text{OOS}}$ values at the portfolio level (27--37\%) are higher than individual stock $R^2_{\text{OOS}}$ values (typically 0.5--3\% in the literature) because portfolio aggregation concentrates the predictable signal.
Individual-stock estimation via Nystr\"{o}m approximation with $m = 500$ landmark points is computationally feasible and is reserved for the extended version of the paper.

Our baseline uses temporal validation on the last 24 months of the training window.
The optimal theory family selected by this procedure is consistent with leave-one-year-out CV on a subsample of windows: in both cases, the winning theory family varies across periods in the same qualitative pattern (demand systems early, volatility and macro in the middle, behavioral during crises).
